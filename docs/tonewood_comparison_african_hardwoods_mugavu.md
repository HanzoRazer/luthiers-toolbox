## Analysis

### The Density Spectrum

These seven species span a 360 kg/m³ density range — from lightweight Khaya (530) to heavyweight Bubinga (890). This isn't just weight; it's a tonal gradient:

| Density Band | Species | Tonal Character |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Light (530–550)** | Khaya, Honduran | Classic mahogany warmth, balanced response, lightweight bodies |
| **Medium (630–650)** | Utile, Sapele | Mahogany-adjacent but brighter, more midrange definition |
| **Heavy-Medium (760–820)** | Mugavu, Ovangkol | Transition zone — warmth plus complexity, rosewood influence |
| **Heavy (890)** | Bubinga | Rich bass, articulate mids, density adds sustain and projection |

### The Mahogany Replacements: Khaya vs Sapele

**African Mahogany (Khaya)** is the closest drop-in replacement for Honduran Mahogany by physical properties — nearly identical density (530 vs 545 kg/m³), matching Janka hardness (830 vs 800), and "similar to Honduran, slightly brighter" tone character. Khaya is actually a Swietenia relative (same family: Meliaceae), which explains the similarity. The sustainability advantage is clear: Khaya is sustainable while Honduran is CITES II.

**Sapele** is the industry-standard mahogany alternative and for good reason — abundant supply, established sustainability, and predictable working properties. At 640 kg/m³, it's 17% denser than Honduran, which shifts the voice: "like mahogany but brighter, good midrange definition." The ribbon figure is visually distinctive. Trade-off: higher tearout tendency (0.50) on that interlocked grain.

### The Mugavu Opportunity

**Mugavu (Albizia coriaria)** occupies the gap between Sapele and Bubinga — and that gap is underserved. At SG 0.76 / 764 kg/m³:

- **19% denser than Sapele** — more mass, more sustain potential
- **14% lighter than Bubinga** — easier to work, lighter instruments
- **Not CITES-listed** — no paperwork burden, simpler export
- **Abundant East African supply** — Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania
- **Existing export infrastructure** — European furniture market already uses it

The acoustic profile is the critical unknown. The estimated impedance (~3.33 × 10⁶ kg/m²s) positions it between Sapele (2.78) and Ovangkol (3.88), suggesting a voice that bridges mahogany warmth and rosewood complexity.

**Regional validation exists:** East African luthiers already use Mugavu as a mahogany replacement. The working properties are documented — interlocked grain requires sharp tooling and climb cuts, similar to Sapele.

### The Fabaceae Woods: Ovangkol and Bubinga

**Ovangkol** and **Bubinga** are both *Guibourtia* species (Fabaceae family), and their tonal character reflects that lineage — "warm, complex, rosewood family character." They're denser, stiffer (MOE 18.4–18.6 GPa vs mahogany's 12.0 GPa), and produce instruments with more projection and sustain.

Bubinga's CITES Appendix II listing (2017) is the complication. Documentation requirements add friction to every transaction. Ovangkol remains unlisted and is the practical alternative for that density/tone profile.

|---------|:---------:|----------------|
| **Ovangkol** | 23.0 | Highest — most efficient energy transfer per gram |

Ovangkol's high E/ρ ratio explains its rosewood-like character — it moves energy efficiently despite the mass. Mugavu's estimated ratio suggests it could compete in this tier with proper selection.

## The Mugavu Pipeline: Next Steps

The memory from the previous session identified Mugavu as a strategic target. The pipeline to production:

### 1. Acoustic Characterization (Highest Priority)

Measured data needed:
- Speed of sound (tap tone or resonance method)
- Acoustic impedance
- Damping coefficient (Q factor)
- MOE/MOR from physical samples

Current values are estimated from SG correlation — measured data will either validate the positioning or reveal surprises.

### 2. Document East African Working Techniques

East African luthiers have solved the interlocked-grain problem. Document:
- Preferred grain orientation
- Tool selection (HSS vs carbide)
- Optimal moisture content
- Finishing compatibility

### 3. Builder Trials in Western Markets

Prototype instruments using Mugavu for:
- Back/sides (most direct Sapele replacement)
- Solid body (density suits electric applications)
- Neck laminates (if MOE validates stiffness)

### 4. Supply Chain Development

European furniture channels exist — pivot to tonewood grading:
- Identify suppliers with quarter-sawing capability
- Establish tonewood grading criteria (grain runout, figure, defect tolerance)
- Trial shipments to US/EU luthier suppliers

## Key Takeaways

1. **Khaya is the closest Honduran Mahogany replacement** — nearly identical properties, no CITES burden.

2. **Sapele is the industry workhorse** — proven, available, sustainable, slightly brighter than mahogany.

3. **Mugavu fills the Sapele–Bubinga gap** — denser than Sapele, lighter than Bubinga, unlisted, abundant supply.

4. **Acoustic characterization is the blocking task** — Mugavu's promise is based on estimated values. Measured speed-of-sound and impedance will validate or invalidate the positioning.

5. **The CITES trajectory favors unlisted species** — Mugavu and Ovangkol have regulatory advantages that will compound over time.

6. **East African validation exists** — regional luthiers already use Mugavu as a mahogany alternative. This is not speculation; it's established practice that needs documentation for Western adoption.

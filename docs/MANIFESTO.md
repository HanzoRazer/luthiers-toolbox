# The Production Shop — Manifesto

---

## You Already Know How to Make Things

You have built furniture that fits together without gaps.
You have run a CNC that holds tolerance to a tenth of a millimeter.
You have designed in CAD, printed in resin, welded, soldered, and finished.
You understand grain direction, feed rate, tool deflection, and cure time.

You have never built a guitar.

Not because you couldn't.
Because nobody made it look like something you could.

---

## The Guild Has a Problem

The luthiery tradition is one of the oldest making traditions in the world.
It is also one of the most deliberately opaque.

The masters will tell you it takes fifty guitars to learn how to build one.
They learned in apprenticeships that no longer exist,
from masters who guarded their methods like trade secrets,
in a tradition that confused difficulty with quality
and gatekeeping with craft.

They are not wrong that it takes fifty guitars.
They built those fifty guitars without the tools that exist today.

---

## The Art Is in the Decisions

There is a false argument in lutherie that goes like this:
hand-built means authentic, CNC means industrial, science means soulless.

It is wrong.

The art was never in the hand plane.
The art was in knowing where to take the wood,
how thin to graduate the top,
what frequency the plate needed to ring at,
which bracing pattern served the player's touch,
how to voice an instrument for the music it would make.

That is the art. That is the craft. That is the intuition.

The tool that removes the wood is not the artist.
The builder who decides how much to remove — that is the artist.

A luthier who understands why a top needs to be 2.8mm at the bridge
and 2.4mm at the waist makes better decisions than one who does not.
That is not less craft. That is more craft.
Because the decisions are informed.

## Fifty Guitars Compressed Into Ten

Every master luthier built bad guitars.
Tops that cracked. Necks that shifted. Instruments that sounded right
and played wrong, or played right and sounded like furniture.

Each failure taught them something their hands remembered.
Fifty repetitions of feedback, stored in muscle memory and judgment.

The Production Shop is what those fifty guitars taught them,
encoded into software.

The physics of tone — Helmholtz resonance, flexural rigidity,
plate stiffness, brace geometry — made readable, interactive, actionable.

The geometry of form — body proportions, scale length, neck angle,
saddle compensation — calculated from first principles,
not copied from a template someone copied from someone else.

The feedback loop between measurement and intuition —
tap the plate, measure the frequency, compare to the target,
adjust the graduation, tap again.
Every iteration logged. Every deviation noted.
Build three benefits from builds one and two.
Build ten benefits from all of them.

You still have to develop the craft.
You still have to learn to hear what the wood is telling you.
You still have to develop the touch that comes only from repetition.

But you do not have to waste forty guitars getting there.

## A New Generation of Builders

The retiring generation of luthiers learned without feedback loops.
They built in isolation, shared reluctantly, and measured success
in decades of experience and the reputation of their name.

You are not that builder.

You share your process. You document your builds.
You iterate faster than any previous generation of makers.
You are comfortable with software, with physics, with data.
You already know that understanding why something works
makes you better at making it work.

You just need the tools that speak your language.

Tools that show you the output before they show you the inputs.
Tools that explain the physics in plain language, not academic notation.
Tools that treat you as the intelligent maker you already are.
Tools that make your tenth guitar as good as someone else's fiftieth.

## What We Built

A platform that does not replace the craft.
A platform that accelerates it.

**Blueprint Reader** — photograph a plan, a template, a sketch.
Get a verified DXF file that opens in your CAM software.
Not a file that looks right. A file that works.

**Acoustic Design Studio** — the physics of tone made interactive.
Soundhole size, plate stiffness, bracing geometry, Helmholtz resonance.
Not formulas to solve. Decisions to make, with the science behind them.

**Rosette Designer** — the most visible signature of the maker.
Design your pattern. See it rendered. Get a BOM and a DXF.
Know before you cut whether the design can actually be built.

**CAM Workstation** — from outline to toolpath to G-code.
For the CNC that sits in your shop.
For the builder who understands that a machine that holds tolerance
is a tool in service of the art, not a threat to it.

**RMOS** — because a safety-critical process deserves a safety system.
Risk assessment before every cut.
Audit trail for every run.
Because the fifty-year-old luthier who lost a finger
learned a lesson you do not have to repeat.

## The Body Is the Cabinet

A guitar body is not just a shape.
It is a speaker enclosure.

Speaker engineers start with a target frequency
and calculate the box. The port size follows from
the volume. The geometry serves the acoustics.

Most luthiers work in reverse — copy a proven shape,
hope the plate voicing compensates, build fifty
guitars until intuition fills the gaps.

The Production Shop works like the speaker engineer.
Draw your body outline. The system calculates the
internal air volume. The inverse solver tells you
what soundhole size hits your target resonance.
The geometry serves the acoustics.
This is what the connected platform means.

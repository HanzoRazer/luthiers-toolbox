## Individual Species Analysis

### 1. Khaya (African Mahogany)

**Viability: HIGH — Production Ready**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | Closest physical match to Honduran Mahogany (density 530 vs 545); same botanical family (Meliaceae); established supply chain; no CITES restrictions; "similar to Honduran, slightly brighter" tone |
| **Weaknesses** | Missing MOE/MOR data in database; slightly more variable than Honduran |
| **Best Applications** | Body, neck — direct 1:1 Honduran replacement |
| **CNC Behavior** | Standard mahogany parameters; hardness scale 0.42; medium tearout risk on interlocked grain |
| **Recommendation** | **Adopt immediately.** No blocking issues. The safe mahogany alternative. |

---

### 2. African Padauk

**Viability: HIGH — Production Ready**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | Exceptional tone ("most promising rosewood replacement" — Dana Bourgeois); high stiffness (MOE 13.07 GPa); low damping = rings like marimba; warm balanced voice with brilliant highs; excellent machining (hardness scale 0.21, low tearout) |
| **Weaknesses** | Vivid orange color fades to brown with UV exposure; can be difficult to bend for acoustic sides; dust is moderately hazardous |
| **Best Applications** | Back/sides (acoustic), body (electric), fretboard — rosewood alternative positioning |
| **CNC Behavior** | Very machine-friendly despite density; chipload multiplier 1.13; max DOC 15mm; low burn/tearout risk |
| **Tone Profile** | Warm, resonant, clear midrange; glassy tap tone; deep bass with precise highs; high Q factor (low damping) |
| **Recommendation** | **Adopt for rosewood-alternative applications.** Color change is cosmetic, not structural. Excellent acoustic properties. |

---

### 3. Mugavu

**Viability: MEDIUM-HIGH — Pending Characterization**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | Strategic positioning between Sapele (640) and Bubinga (890) at 764 kg/m³; same genus as Monkeypod but much stiffer; abundant East African supply; not CITES; existing luthier use in East Africa; European furniture export channels exist |
| **Weaknesses** | **All acoustic data is estimated** — no measured speed of sound, impedance, or tap tone; Janka estimated from SG correlation; no Western builder trials documented |
| **Best Applications** | Body, back/sides — heavier mahogany alternative or lighter Bubinga substitute |
| **CNC Behavior** | Estimated similar to Ovangkol; interlocked grain requires climb cuts |
| **Blocking Task** | **Acoustic characterization required before production adoption** |
| **Recommendation** | **High-priority prototype candidate.** Obtain samples, measure acoustic properties, run builder trials. If data validates estimates, this fills an underserved density niche. |

---

### 4. American Sycamore

**Viability: MEDIUM — Prototype Candidate**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | Mahogany-weight (545 kg/m³) with distinctive quartersawn figure (lace-like ray fleck); easiest machining in this comparison (hardness 0.12, chipload 1.20×, feeds up to 5,400 mm/min); abundant domestic US supply; no restrictions |
| **Weaknesses** | Lowest MOR (69 MPa) — least rigid structurally; acoustic properties computed, not measured; interlocked grain tears on flatsawn faces; no established lutherie track record |
| **Best Applications** | Back/sides (mahogany weight class); experimental soundboard (low density); visual accent pieces |
| **CNC Behavior** | Extremely forgiving — lowest burn tendency (0.16), highest max DOC (16mm), fastest feeds |
| **Tone Profile** | Estimated warm, open, airy with pronounced mids and low damping (based on MOE/density) |
| **Recommendation** | **Worth prototyping for visual appeal and easy workability.** Quartersawn figure is unique among North American hardwoods. Needs measured acoustic data. |

---

### 5. Monkeypod

**Viability: MEDIUM-HIGH — Luthier Endorsed**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | **Bob Taylor: "I love monkey pod"**; rated 4.0/5 tone by Tonewood Data Source; "between Koa and Mahogany" character; rich harmonics with strong bass (4.4/5); warm, mellow, intimate voice; beautiful figured grain (golden amber to chocolate browns); easy machining; abundant tropical supply |
| **Weaknesses** | Lower projection ("not loud") and below-average sustain vs rosewood/maple — a tonal character, not a defect; supply chain less established than koa; wide pores require pore filling |
| **Best Applications** | Back/sides (acoustic), body (electric/acoustic), neck — anywhere warm Koa-like tone is desired |
| **CNC Behavior** | Easy — hardness 0.14, low burn/tearout risk, similar to Sycamore |
| **Tone Profile** | Warm, mellow, balanced; strong bass presence; smooth trebles; rich harmonics; intimate rather than projecting — sits between Koa and Mahogany |
| **Comparison to Mugavu** | Same genus (*Albizia*) but different voice — Monkeypod is warmer/mellower, Mugavu is denser/stiffer. Both are viable but for different tonal goals. |
| **Recommendation** | **Adopt for warm-voiced builds.** The low MOE creates the mellow character luthiers prize, not a structural problem. Supply chain is the only real barrier (per Bob Taylor). |

---

### 6. Bagtikan (White Seraya)

**Viability: MEDIUM — Source from Malaysia**

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| **Strengths** | **Major Malaysian export timber** — 2nd largest from Sabah; IUCN Least Concern (2019); abundant in Sabah lowland forests (up to 70% of commercial trees); established "White Seraya" trade name; machines easily; mid-range specimens have adequate structural properties |
| **Weaknesses** | **High variability** — density spans 390–670 kg/m³ (72% range), requiring individual blank grading; lower-end specimens too soft; Philippines population is threatened (don't source there); tonally described as "balanced and bland" |
| **Best Applications** | Back/sides, body — mahogany weight class when properly graded (select 550+ kg/m³) |
| **CNC Behavior** | Easy — similar to softer mahoganies; low burn/tearout risk |
| **Sourcing** | **Malaysia/Sabah only** as "White Seraya" — avoid Philippine sources where population is stressed |
| **Tone Profile** | Neutral, balanced, moderate sustain — functional but unremarkable; similar to light red meranti family |
| **Recommendation** | **Viable if graded carefully.** Source from Malaysian suppliers as White Seraya. Reject blanks below 550 kg/m³. Not a standout tonewood, but a workable mahogany-class option with established supply. |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Khaya is the safe bet** — production-ready Honduran Mahogany replacement with no blocking issues.

2. **African Padauk is the rosewood play** — Dana Bourgeois endorsement, excellent acoustic properties, machine-friendly. Color fading is cosmetic.

3. **Mugavu is the strategic opportunity** — fills the Sapele-Bubinga gap, abundant supply, but needs acoustic validation before commitment.

4. **American Sycamore is the wild card** — unique visual appeal, easiest machining, but unproven acoustically.

5. **Monkeypod is luthier-approved** — Bob Taylor loves it; rated 4.0/5 tone. The low MOE creates warmth, not weakness. "Between Koa and Mahogany" positioning. Supply chain is the only barrier.

6. **Bagtikan is viable from Malaysia** — IUCN downgraded to Least Concern (2019); major Sabah export as "White Seraya." High variability requires grading (reject <550 kg/m³). Avoid Philippine sources.

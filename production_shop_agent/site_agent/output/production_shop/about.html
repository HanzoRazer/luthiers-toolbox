About Us

Built by a luthier,
engineered for production.

This isn't software built by someone who read about guitar making. It was written late at night, in a shop full of sawdust and G-code, by someone who needed it to actually work.

How it started — and why it matters.

I spent the first decade of my career as a licensed Professional Engineer doing hydraulic modeling — fluid dynamics, pipeline networks, the kind of work where the math has to be exactly right or something breaks. I used tools like EPANET, ran simulations, validated outputs. Precision wasn't a preference; it was a professional requirement.

Then I started building guitars. What started as a weekend hobby became an obsession, and the obsession turned into a small production operation. I was running a CNC router, managing a parts inventory, quoting custom orders, and trying to keep track of which neck profile went with which body blank for which customer. The craft side of things felt natural. The business side was chaos.

I looked at every tool on the market. ERP systems designed for furniture factories — overkill and completely ignorant of instrument geometry. Lutherie-specific apps — beautifully designed for one-off builds, totally useless at 10 or 20 guitars a month. CAM software — powerful, but completely siloed from my order list, my wood stock, or my customer records.

So I built my own. The Production Shop started as a collection of Python scripts duct-taped together with spreadsheets. Over two years, it became something I'd actually want to sell to another builder. It speaks the language of lutherie — neck relief, scale length, fret slot geometry — while also handling the language of production: job costing, machine scheduling, material yield, margin tracking.

If you've ever quoted a guitar order on the back of a sticky note, you'll understand why this exists.

Our Mission

One platform for the
whole instrument business.

The Production Shop exists to give independent instrument makers the same operational discipline as a modern manufacturing company — without making them think like one. We believe that craft and precision aren't in conflict. When a luthier has clear visibility into their costs, their machine time, and their material stock, they make better guitars, price them more confidently, and build a business that sustains the work they love. That's what we're here for.

Under the Hood

Engineering-grade precision, applied to lutherie.

The computational backbone of The Production Shop draws directly from the methods I used in hydraulic engineering — iterative solvers, tolerance-aware calculations, and validation layers that flag inconsistencies before they reach the shop floor. The same rigor that models a water distribution network is what ensures your fret slot positions are correct to four decimal places.

What We Stand For

Built on three ideas.

These aren't marketing words. They're the principles that shaped every product decision we've made.

01

Craft First

The software serves the build, not the other way around. Every UI decision starts with the question: does this make the work of instrument making easier, or does it just make the software look busier? We build for builders, which means we hold the tool to the same standard you'd apply to a hand plane — it should do exactly what it's supposed to do, without fighting you.

02

Engineering Precision

Tolerances matter. Whether it's a fret slot at the 22nd position or a material cost variance in a job costing report, approximate isn't good enough. We apply the same computational discipline to lutherie that civil engineers apply to infrastructure — not because we're showing off, but because imprecision has a cost, and that cost usually lands on the builder.

03

Builder Community

The best ideas in this platform came from conversations with other builders — people I've met at trade shows, in online forums, or who emailed me after reading a post I wrote about jig design. The Production Shop isn't developed in isolation. If you have a workflow problem, a feature idea, or a better way to think about something, we genuinely want to hear it. That feedback loop is how good tools get made.
